# Narrative Revision Plan

## 1. Revision Purpose

This document is the master plan for revising the thesis from a guideline-based knowledge-representation study into a comparative study of Rule-Based and Decision Tree classification.

The revised narrative must consistently communicate four facts:

1. The target classes are `Urgent` and `Not Urgent`.
2. The ground truth comes from historical company administrator decisions.
3. Rule-Based classification is constructed from company SOP through Knowledge Acquisition and Rule Construction.
4. Decision Tree classification is trained from historical invoice attributes and administrator labels.

The primary contribution is Comparative Analysis. Knowledge Engineering is retained as a supporting methodology for constructing and validating the Rule-Based method.

## 2. Revision Boundary

This plan may be used to revise Chapters I-V, but numerical results must not be written until the administrator-labeled dataset has passed the data-readiness gate in Section 9.

The following legacy evidence must not be reused as evidence for the revised study:

- labels `HIGH`, `MEDIUM`, and `NORMAL`;
- Guideline-Based Ground Truth;
- perfect metrics produced against guideline-derived labels;
- Decision Tree reconstruction claims;
- Knowledge Representation Consistency Evaluation results; and
- conclusions based on the Operational Knowledge Formalization Framework as the primary contribution.

Legacy files may remain in the repository for traceability, but the current thesis chapters must not cite them as results of the new experiment.

## 3. New Core Narrative

The thesis investigates whether a Rule-Based method derived from company SOP and a Decision Tree method learned from historical administrator decisions produce different classification performance and operational characteristics when evaluated against the same administrator ground truth.

The Rule-Based branch begins with Knowledge Acquisition. The company SOP is examined to identify urgency conditions, thresholds, exceptions, and rule precedence. These findings are transformed through Rule Construction and Rule Validation into a deterministic classifier that outputs `Urgent` or `Not Urgent`.

The Decision Tree branch begins with the verified historical dataset. The model is trained using invoice attributes and administrator labels within each experimental training partition. Pruning and model selection are performed without access to final test labels. The resulting model outputs `Urgent` or `Not Urgent` and provides interpretable paths and feature importance.

Both branches converge in Comparative Evaluation. Their predictions are compared with administrator ground truth on the same invoice cases. The analysis includes performance metrics, stability across validation scenarios, error patterns, invoice-level case studies, explainability, adaptability, maintainability, scalability, computational cost, ease of updating, and SOP compliance.

## 6. Proposed Chapter I Foundation

Chapter I should establish the following argument:

1. Invoice urgency is an operational decision that affects document handling and delivery priorities.
2. Company SOP provides explicit decision knowledge, while historical administrator decisions provide empirical decision outcomes.
3. Rule-Based and Decision Tree offer two interpretable but methodologically different classification approaches.
4. Earlier evaluation based on guideline-generated labels cannot provide an independent comparison.
5. The research gap is the absence of a controlled comparison against administrator ground truth.
6. The research objective is to compare performance, stability, error patterns, and operational characteristics.

Possible working-title direction:

> Comparative Analysis of Rule-Based and Decision Tree Methods for Invoice Urgency Classification Using Administrator Decisions as Ground Truth

The final title must follow institutional language and formatting requirements.

## 10. Experimental Design

| ID | Scenario | Decision Tree procedure | Rule-Based procedure | Evaluation output |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| E1 | Stratified hold-out 80:20 | Train and prune on the 80% training portion; predict the 20% test portion. | Apply frozen rules to the identical 20% test invoices. | Test confusion matrices, metrics, and paired prediction table. |
| E2 | Stratified hold-out 70:30 | Train and prune on the 70% training portion; predict the 30% test portion. | Apply frozen rules to the identical 30% test invoices. | Test confusion matrices, metrics, and paired prediction table. |
| E3 | Stratified 5-fold cross-validation | Train in each fold and generate one out-of-fold prediction for every invoice. | Apply frozen rules to each corresponding validation fold. | Aggregated out-of-fold confusion matrices, metrics, fold stability, and paired predictions. |
| E4 | LOOCV | Train on all but one invoice and predict the held-out invoice, repeated for every invoice. | Apply frozen rules to each held-out invoice. | Aggregated out-of-fold confusion matrices, metrics, and paired predictions. |

Experimental controls:

1. Use stratification for E1-E3 when each class has enough observations.
2. Record fixed random seeds for hold-out and fold generation.
3. Use the same held-out invoice identifiers for both methods.
4. Fit train-dependent preprocessing only on training data.
5. Select Decision Tree pruning or hyperparameters without using final test labels.
6. Aggregate LOOCV predictions before computing precision, recall, and F1-score.
7. Preserve every case-level prediction with invoice ID, ground truth, both outputs, rule trace, and tree path where feasible.
8. If perfect results occur, audit leakage, duplicates, target-derived features, rule-label equivalence, and split construction before interpreting the result.
9. Do not select seeds based on favorable outcomes.
10. If a paired significance test is used, apply a paired method such as McNemar's test to predictions on the same cases and report its assumptions and limited power with a small sample.

### 4.7 Misclassification Case Studies

- only Rule-Based correct;
- only Decision Tree correct; and
- both methods incorrect.

## 16. Final Revision Principle

The revised thesis must be understandable as a comparative study within ten seconds of reading its title, research framework, objectives, and Chapter IV headings.
